Avatar Selection Interface

Unity Developer for PSTechnology

PST sells Rail Management and Training products to Class 1 and 2 Railroads throughout North America. As such, their softwares are used by a diverse demographic. However, their interfaces are not designed to reflect this diversity. In the Training Simulations, the avatar controlled by the trainee has been the default legacy model since the initial launch of the training sim.


The default avatar, "Signalman"

Representation in media and education is incredibly important, and has shown to contribute to increased content retention. As such, I was tasked to create an interface through which customers could select their own avatar from a settings panel. Additionally, I needed to design a system to dynamically update and save that local configuration internally, so that users only needed to select their avatar one time.


A simple button to access the selector, containing the user's current avatar.

I created a simple window to handle the interaction of editing the avatar. The window dynamically resizes according to the number of avatars available to the user. In addition, the tool automatically disables if there is only one available avatar, so clients can opt into this feature depending on their needs.


A simple window to select between two avatars.
An expanded window to hold up to 8 avatars.

The last problem I needed to consider was being able to resize the profile images of the avatars in the selector. I needed to be able to downsize the current profile image to become the button on the main panel, and I needed to be able to scale up images to fit on a large screen. This is because training Simulations run on a variety of devices. I created a custom bilinier interpolation implementation to handle this.


Current Project Status

The selector is currently functional for displaying up to 8 avatars. After the selector is utilized, the correct Avatar model is correctly loaded into the Simulation. The project to create the avatar models is currently underway. When the new models are ready, they will be imported into the build and released to current customers. When we need to expand the selector to hold more than 8 avatars, I will implement a paging system to allow users to click through their options.

    Raise Your Hands Interactive Exhibit

    Projection & Set Design Lead

    Technologies: Unity, C#, Media Pipe in Python, Adobe Photoshop & Illustrator

    Raise Your Hands is an interactive art exhibit lead by a team of students and faculty at Georgia Institute of Technology. Students with Computing, Engineering, Industial Design and Music Tech backgrounds created a space where participant motion causes various real-time reactions in the exhibit. This included music generation, mechatronics movement, projectoral animations and small lighting effects.


    View an explanation of our exhibit here!


    Our exhibit consisted of three sections enclosed in a wooden frame. Each section had a camera that tracked motion and sent the collected data through UDP communication to control the interactive elements.


    An abstact 2D scene in Section 1

    This section solely responded to hand raise data, and manipulated the sprite creation and positioning rates accordingly.


    A cel-shaded forest scene for Section 2

    This section generated bird flocks in response to hand height and manipulated the wind in the environment in reponse to hand height.


    A low-poly nighttime forest scene for Section 3

    This section responded to hand raise data as well as head shift data to disorient the user. Participants were able to travel up and down the forest paths, and make the mushrooms in the scene spin and glow.



    In addition, the projection team received LIDAR data to detect whether participants had come too close to the exhibit artifacts, in order to maintain their safety. We displayed a simple intrusion alert on the forward-facing projection screen.


    Our Intrusion Alert Banner


    My team's main focus was to develop the projections, and design and construct the set. In addition to creating the projections, my main role was to create a UDP receiver to create an access point for the Unity assets models to pull motion data from. After parsing JSON data retreived from our team's specified port, we outputted the data points we needed to public-access variables. We utilized thread locks(mutex locks) when updating the variables to prevent race conditions.


    My favorite experience with the exhibit was watching people interact with our work. This family came across our exhibit by accident, but had time to come by. They spent about 20 minutes walking in and out of the exhibit, and watching their excitement while investigating their surroundings was a special memory. This video was taken with their permission.


    If the embed doesn't work, watch the clip here


    Current Project Status

    Raise Your Hands ran for two weeks in November of 2022, we received a lot of feedback from students, faculty and visitors to the performing arts center in which we held the exhibit. The team has also received extra funding from Georgia Tech, so we will be expanding the project in all aspects. We are in communication with schools, the Atlanta Botanical Gardens and Georgia Tech to set up mini exhibits, or even collaborate to create learning and experimental tools so that students can create their own projects using motion tracking software.

Product Common Features Project

FullStack Developer

Tech: TypeScript, HTML/CSS, Node.JS, Java, Spring Boot, PostgreSQL, DBeaver, SQL, Figma, LucidChart


  • The Problem

    This project was a 2-month explorative project, where me and my partner Rahi designed a simple web app to serve as a proof-of-concept for a larger restructuring project within our department. PST creates many software products for its vast clientele, but each client has different needs. As such, the dev team spends countless hours in production integrating client preferences into their applications. Many of these customers purchase multiple apps, but there is currently no way to dynamically pull in their preferences into all the products they purchase. Rather, the dev team has to copy over the same data to any new product they purchase. This process is repetitive and results in a waste of time and subsequently revenue.


    The Solution

    Currently, client preferences, or configurations, are stored in large text files. We proposed to move this data to the cloud. This way, our clients can access their configurations from anywhere! To give clients a route to access their configurations, we decided to create a simple web-app that clients could interface to edit their preferences on their own time. By shifting the load of configuration integration to the customer, we can focus our time on developing new products and expanding existing ones. This app would allow clients to upload not only generic info such as company branding, but also allow them to upload and edit their own JSON configurations, language translations, etc, to integrate our base products with the rest of their technology and language/railroad conventions. For example, different Rail companies use different naming conventions for their scheduling systems. Our web-app would allow them to input their own conventions in place of defaults. Without a product like this, our own dev team would have to manually change each word or variable in the software, and repeat this process for all products purchased by that client.
